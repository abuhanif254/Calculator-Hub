## The Flaw in Manual Time Tracking

The human brain is naturally wired to think in base-10 (decimals). However, our clocks operate on a base-60 system (60 seconds in a minute, 60 minutes in an hour). This structural mismatch makes manual time calculation incredibly unintuitive. 

If you clock in at 8:15 AM and clock out at 4:45 PM, a quick mental calculation might lead you to believe you worked 8.30 hours. This is mathematically incorrect. 
Because minutes operate in base-60, 45 minutes minus 15 minutes is 30 minutes. 30 minutes is exactly half an hour, which translates to **0.50** in decimal format. You worked 8.50 hours, not 8.30. 

When you multiply these tiny conversion errors across a two-week pay period, you end up severely shortchanging yourself or your employees.

## The Crucial Conversion: Minutes to Decimals

If you are filling out a corporate timesheet or generating a freelance invoice in Quickbooks, you cannot submit your time as "8 hours and 15 minutes." Payroll software requires decimal format.

To convert minutes to decimals manually, you must divide the number of minutes by 60.
*   15 minutes $\div 60$ = **.25 hours**
*   30 minutes $\div 60$ = **.50 hours**
*   45 minutes $\div 60$ = **.75 hours**

### The "7-Minute Rule" in Payroll
Many corporate HR departments use standard time-rounding software based on the Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA). This is often called the 7-Minute Rule (or the 1/10th of an hour rule). 

Under this rule, time is rounded to the nearest 15-minute increment (.25).
*   If you clock in at 8:07 AM, it rounds **down** to 8:00 AM (in your favor).
*   If you clock in at 8:08 AM, it rounds **up** to 8:15 AM (against you).
Our Hours Calculator provides exact, unrounded totals, allowing you to compare your true time against your employer's rounded payroll system to ensure they are applying the federal rounding rules fairly.

## Understanding Overtime Thresholds

If you are an hourly (non-exempt) employee in the United States, tracking your hours accurately is critical because it triggers mandatory overtime pay. 

Under federal law (FLSA), any time worked over **40 hours in a single, 7-day workweek** must be paid at "Time and a Half" (1.5$\times$ your standard hourly rate). 

*Crucial Caveat:* Federal law is based on the *week*, not the *day*. If you work a 12-hour shift on Monday, you are not entitled to overtime for those extra 4 hours *unless* your total hours for the entire week exceed 40. 

*State Exceptions:* Certain states, like California, have far stricter daily overtime laws. In California, any time worked over **8 hours in a single day** triggers time and a half, regardless of the weekly total. If you work in a state with daily overtime, using our calculator to track your precise daily totals is absolutely essential to ensure your paycheck is legally compliant.
