## What is Volume?

Volume is the quantity of three-dimensional space enclosed by a closed boundary, such as the space that a substance (solid, liquid, gas, or plasma) occupies or contains. It is the three-dimensional equivalent of length (one-dimensional) and area (two-dimensional). 

Volume is often quantified numerically using the SI-derived unit, the **cubic meter ($m^3$)**, or other imperial/US units like the **cubic foot ($ft^3$)**, **cubic yard ($yd^3$)**, and capacity units such as **liters ($L$)** and **gallons ($gal$)**.

## Volume Formulas for the 14 Shapes

The calculator handles 14 separate geometric shapes, each governed by its own mathematical formula. Below is an educational breakdown of how these shapes are calculated.

### 1. Cube
A cube is a three-dimensional solid object bounded by six square faces, facets or sides, with three meeting at each vertex.
*   **Formula:** $V = a^3$
*   *where $a$ is the edge length.*

### 2. Rectangular Prism (Cuboid)
A rectangular prism is a polyhedral shape with six faces that are rectangles.
*   **Formula:** $V = l \cdot w \cdot h$
*   *where $l$ is length, $w$ is width, and $h$ is height.*

### 3. Cylinder
A cylinder is a solid geometric figure with straight parallel sides and a circular or oval cross-section.
*   **Formula:** $V = \pi r^2 h$
*   *where $r$ is the base radius and $h$ is the height.*

### 4. Sphere
A sphere is a round geometrical object in three-dimensional space that is the surface of a completely round ball.
*   **Formula:** $V = \frac{4}{3} \pi r^3$
*   *where $r$ is the radius.*

### 5. Cone
A cone is a three-dimensional geometric shape that tapers smoothly from a flat base to a point called the apex.
*   **Formula:** $V = \frac{1}{3} \pi r^2 h$
*   *where $r$ is the base radius and $h$ is the height.*

### 6. Pyramid
A pyramid is a polyhedron formed by connecting a polygonal base and a point, called the apex.
*   **Formula:** $V = \frac{1}{3} A_{base} h$
*   *where $A_{base}$ is the area of the base (length $\times$ width for a rectangular base) and $h$ is the height.*

### 7. Triangular Prism
A triangular prism is a three-sided prism; it is a polyhedron made of a triangular base, a translated copy, and 3 faces joining corresponding sides.
*   **Formula:** $V = \frac{1}{2} b \cdot h_{base} \\cdot H$
*   *where $b$ is the triangle base width, $h_{base}$ is the triangle base height, and $H$ is the vertical height of the prism.*

### 8. Ellipsoid
An ellipsoid is a surface that may be obtained from a sphere by deforming it by means of directional scalings.
*   **Formula:** $V = \frac{4}{3} \pi a b c$
*   *where $a$, $b$, and $c$ are the lengths of the three semi-axes.*

### 9. Capsule
A capsule is a basic 3D geometric shape consisting of a cylinder with hemispherical ends (caps).
*   **Formula:** $V = \pi r^2 \left(\frac{4}{3} r + h_{cyl}\right)$
*   *where $r$ is the radius and $h_{cyl}$ is the height of the cylindrical section.*

### 10. Torus
A torus is a surface of revolution generated by revolving a circle in three-dimensional space about an axis coplanar with the circle.
*   **Formula:** $V = 2 \pi^2 R r^2$
*   *where $R$ is the major radius (distance from the center of the tube to the center of the torus) and $r$ is the minor radius (radius of the tube).*

### 11. Hemisphere
A hemisphere is half of a sphere.
*   **Formula:** $V = \frac{2}{3} \pi r^3$

### 12. Cone Frustum
A frustum is the portion of a cone that lies between two parallel planes cutting it.
*   **Formula:** $V = \frac{1}{3} \pi h (R^2 + Rr + r^2)$
*   *where $R$ is the bottom radius, $r$ is the top radius, and $h$ is the height.*

### 13. Hollow Cylinder (Cylindrical Shell)
A hollow cylinder is a cylinder with a smaller cylinder removed from its core.
*   **Formula:** $V = \pi (R^2 - r^2) h$
*   *where $R$ is the outer radius, $r$ is the inner radius, and $h$ is the height.*

### 14. Hollow Sphere (Spherical Shell)
A hollow sphere is a sphere with a concentric inner spherical core removed.
*   **Formula:** $V = \frac{4}{3} \pi (R^3 - r^3)$
*   *where $R$ is the outer radius and $r$ is the inner radius.*

In real-world construction, logistics, and physics calculations, you often need to perform calculations using multiple unit systems. 

For example, a contractor might measure a rectangular concrete slab in feet but need the final output in cubic yards to place an order. Alternatively, a chemical engineer might measure tank dimensions in centimeters but require the final volume in US Gallons or Liters.

*   **1 US Gallon (gal)** = $0.00378541\text{ m}^3$
