## The Ultimate Guide to Calculating Volume

From pouring concrete for a driveway to filling a swimming pool or designing packaging for a product, understanding how to calculate volume is an essential math skill for construction, manufacturing, and daily life.

Our **Free Volume Calculator** instantly solves the complex geometric equations for the most common 3D shapes, providing you with exact measurements in cubic units.

### What is Volume?

In geometry, **volume** is the measure of the three-dimensional space enclosed within a closed boundary. Put simply, if surface area is the amount of wrapping paper needed to cover a box, volume is the amount of physical space *inside* the box. 

Because it is three-dimensional (length, width, and height/depth), volume is always expressed in cubic units, such as cubic meters (m³), cubic inches (in³), or fluid measurements like liters and gallons.

### Formulas for the 5 Most Common 3D Shapes

If you want to understand the math powering our calculator, here are the standard geometric formulas used to determine the volume of common shapes:

#### 1. Volume of a Rectangular Prism (Box)
This is the simplest and most common volume calculation, used for everything from shipping boxes to rooms in a house.
*   **The Formula:** `V = length × width × height`
*   **Example:** A box that is 10 inches long, 5 inches wide, and 4 inches high has a volume of (10 × 5 × 4) = **200 cubic inches**.

#### 2. Volume of a Cylinder
Cylinders have two parallel circular bases. This formula is crucial for pipes, silos, and beverage cans.
*   **The Formula:** `V = π × r² × h` *(where 'r' is the radius of the circular base and 'h' is the height)*
*   **Example:** A water tank with a radius of 2 meters and a height of 5 meters has a volume of (3.14159 × 2² × 5) = **62.83 cubic meters**.

#### 3. Volume of a Sphere
A sphere is a perfectly round 3D object, like a basketball or a globe. 
*   **The Formula:** `V = 4/3 × π × r³` *(where 'r' is the radius from the center to the edge)*
*   **Example:** A spherical container with a radius of 3 cm has a volume of (4/3 × 3.14159 × 3³) = **113.1 cubic centimeters**.

#### 4. Volume of a Cone
A cone has a circular base that tapers to a point (the apex). Interestingly, a cone is exactly one-third the volume of a cylinder with the same base and height.
*   **The Formula:** `V = 1/3 × π × r² × h`
*   **Example:** An ice cream cone with a radius of 2 inches and a height of 6 inches has a volume of (1/3 × 3.14159 × 2² × 6) = **25.13 cubic inches**.

#### 5. Volume of a Cube
A cube is a special type of rectangular prism where all three sides (length, width, and height) are exactly the same length.
*   **The Formula:** `V = a³` *(where 'a' is the length of one side)*
*   **Example:** A Rubik's Cube with side lengths of 5.7 cm has a volume of (5.7 × 5.7 × 5.7) = **185.19 cubic centimeters**.

### The Difference Between Volume and Capacity

While often used interchangeably, **volume** and **capacity** represent two slightly different concepts in physics. 

*   **Volume** is the amount of physical space an object takes up.
*   **Capacity** is the amount of substance (usually liquid or gas) that a container can hold.

For example, a solid block of iron has volume, but it has zero capacity because it cannot hold anything inside. A hollow water bottle has both volume (the space the plastic itself takes up) and capacity (the 1 liter of water it can hold).
